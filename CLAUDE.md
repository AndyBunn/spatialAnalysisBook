# Writing Style Spec

## Voice and Tone

First person, direct, conversational. Write like you are talking to a smart person in the room, not composing a document for posterity. Warm but not effusive. Trust the reader to keep up.

## Sentence Rhythm

Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones. Use the short sentence for emphasis, for a pivot, for a landing. "Ok." is a full sentence. "That's it." is a full sentence. Don't be afraid of them.

## Rhetorical Questions

Use them. Not constantly, but they are a natural part of how you explain things. "What if your variable isn't as well behaved as a forest stem plot?" pulls the reader forward better than "Consider the case where your variable is not well behaved."

## Humor and Personality

Let it in. Parenthetical asides, mild self-deprecation, enthusiasm for the subject. "The spatstat library has a ludicrous number of datasets." Don't sand it off.

## Practical and Grounded

Signal what matters and what doesn't. Tell readers when something is overkill, when they can skip a section, when you know they'll be tempted to go down a rabbit hole. Be clear about tradeoffs.

## What to Avoid

- No em dashes. Use a comma, a hyphen, or a new sentence instead.
- The words *genuine*, *honest*, and *real* are off limits.
- No constructions of the form "It's not X. It's Y." That pattern is a cliche. If you need to draw a contrast, find another way.
- No over-formatted prose with bullet-point logic where a sentence would do.
- No throat-clearing before you get to the point.
